SKY
Science Cloud Yantra
SKY, for Science-focused Cloud-based Yantra, is at the core of the OSS platform. SKY corresponds to a “science machine” (yantra is a Sanskrit term for a machine). SKY is a general-purpose, Turing-complete machine that allows users to define and execute any science experiment.
OSS is the complete stack around it which encompasses a graphical, drag-and-drop visual interface, a high-level programming language for science experiments, a compiler for translating user-specified high-level logic into lower-level robotic instructions and API calls, a runtime engine that schedules and manages the execution of programs specified by different users, a tracing engine that helps to generate the protocols by reverse engineering the experimental steps that yielded the best results.
Visual Interface
SKY can be accessed in two ways by users:
1. Interactive visual interface
2. Programmatic interface

For education purpose, users can access SKY via a visual interface and execute one instruction at a time. For each command issued by them, the robot performs corresponding action in the real, physical laboratory. With the real-time control of robotic movements and closed feedback loop via video streaming, users can get first-hand feel of running science experiments. For research purpose, users can run experiments in remote, cloud-based labs by expressing the flow of experimental steps using a high-level programming language, which we refer to as PL/SKY. PL/SKY allows users to precisely define experimental workflow, which includes iterations (loops) and conditionals (if-then-else). PL/SKY programs allow users to run a series of experiments in a systematic and automated manner to carefully and efficiently explore the best way to run the experiment.
SKY Instruction Set
SKY is a general-purpose, Turing-complete machine that allows users to define and execute any science experiment. SKY does this with a small set of instructions, which are of three types: Execute, Operate, and Compute instructions.
Execute instructions

Specify instrument inputs digitally (via APIs) or physically (via buttons, knobs, handles, etc.)

With the help of appropriate instruments, operations such as heat, cool, filter, wash, extract, weigh, etc. would be executed

Track the instrument digitally (via APIs) or physically (via video streaming of instrument screens, listening to sounds such as beeps, temperature sensors, etc.)

Record instrument outputs digitally (via APIs) or physically (via screenshot/image of output screens, etc.)

Operate instructions
(instructions executed by robots or human operators)

Pick / Place

Pour / Transfer / Add / Pump (liquids)

Add / Transfer (solids)

Remove / Transfer (liquids / solids)

Twist open / Flip open

Push (button)

Pull / Push (grab an object such as a handle pull or push)

Push (grab an object and push) / Hold (continue an operation for n seconds)

Smear (for example, to spread blood drop on a microscope slide)

Compute instructions

Instructions corresponding to typical programming language constructs such as variables, conditionals, control-flow, loops, exception handling, and so on.

Education

Open Science Labs (OSL) are a network of science labs that provide cloud-based access to science labs to school and college students.

As per Government of India survey, 65% of Class 11th and 12th students in India don’t have access to adequate science labs. There are more than 16 million senior school students studying science in India. Moreover, 71% of Class 9th and 10th students in India don’t have access to adequate science labs. There are more than 38 million high school students studying science in India.

OSL provides anytime and anywhere access to fully-equipped science labs to students. By providing opportunities to students to do science experiments with real equipment, OSL helps to strengthen students’ scientific foundations. OSL enables teachers to fuel the spark of scientific inquiry by guiding interested students to define and run a large variety of science experiments to explore and understand the world around them.

Research
A survey of 1,576 scientists as participants revealed that more than 50% of scientists say that their experiments are not reproducible.
The above results are from Nature's online survey (published in 2016) of 1,576 researchers, who were asked a few questions about reproducibility in research. 52% of those surveyed felt that there was a significant 'crisis' of reproducibility. The right-hand side figure is even more troubling. It shows that more than 70% of researchers failed to reproduce another scientist's experiments. In fact, more than half have failed to repeat and replicate their own experiments!
PL/SKY allows experiments to be expressed as “science programs”, which precisely capture all details related to the execution of experiments. These include experimental parameters as well as operating parameters and conditions.
PL/SKY science programs make it possible for researchers to enable their collaborators to reproduce and replicate their experiments easily. Since the programs encapsulate all details needed to run experiments, others can repeat experiments (to validate past experiments and to collect new observations based on modifications and variations) just by running the programs again. This will help alleviate a major challenge facing the research community right now: the reproducibility crisis!
After replicating the experiments, researchers will also be able to modify and enhance experiments by modifying the operating conditions/parameters of one or more steps or, even, by adding one or more steps. A number of possible variations of the original experiment can be run by adding appropriate loops and conditions in the original PL/SKY program and then running these sets of experiments on OSL in a semi-automated manner with the help of robots and human lab assistants.
Startup
From the startup ecosystem perspective, just like the cloud platforms reduced the CapEx and OpEx for digital-first startups, the OSL platform will reduce the CapEx required by startups (such as those in life sciences, renewable energy, and several other evolving fields) to get started with building and testing products anchored around scientific innovations and core-tech R&D. In addition, OSL will reduce the OpEx required to maintain and manage labs filled with a wide variety of equipment, which requires skilled lab technicians and dedicated support staff for procuring various lab materials (such as reagents, chemicals, etc.).
Partnerships
ARTPARK (AI and Robotics Research Park) is a research centre located at the Indian Institute of Science in Bangalore, India. The centre focuses on research in areas such as robotics, automation, and advanced manufacturing. In the field of robotics, IISc ARTPARK conducts research on various aspects of robotics, including control systems, artificial intelligence, machine learning, and computer vision.

The Systems Biology Institute (SBI) is a research institute based in Tokyo, Japan that focuses on systems biology, a field that combines biology, mathematics, and computer science to study complex biological systems. SBI has an active research program in robotics, which involves developing robots that can assist in biological research and medicine. These robots are designed to perform a range of tasks, such as handling samples, performing experiments, and analyzing data.

About Us
Open Science Stack (OSS) is a not-for-profit initiative building a societal-scale platform for open science. OSS is building the world’s first science computer using open and extensible architecture so that researchers can run any science experiment.
